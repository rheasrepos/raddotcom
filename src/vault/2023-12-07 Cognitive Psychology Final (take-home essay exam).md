Question 1 (Weeks 1-3)
Cognitivism sparked a paradigmatic revolution, the cognitive revolution, in psychology. What
fundamental principle in pre-cognitivism psychology, did the cognitive revolution primarily
challenge?
a.

Cognitivism aimed to reject the reliance on introspective methods of understanding
consciousness and cognitive processes.
b. Cognitivism disputed the idea that observable behavior should be included in our study of
psychological processes and, instead, sought to focus only on the significance of mental
processes and the study of the mind.
c. Cognitivism aimed to challenge the dominance of behaviorism in psychological
study because of how behaviorisms severely limited the study of cognition through
the exclusion of the mind in psychological inquiry.
d. Cognitivism took issue with Tolman’s findings that behavior is strengthened through
positive reinforcement in his experiments on rats and posited that operant conditioning
could not be explained through behavioral observation.
Explanation:

This question hopes that the student recognizes that the cognitive revolution aimed to
reintroduce the study of the mind into psychology after Watson’s founding of behaviorism
rejected that consciousness was a viable area of study in psychology (pg. 10, textbook). The key
word in the question is “primarily” and hints that some answers may be partially correct but not
the best answer to the question.
Option A is misleading because it correctly questions the reliability of introspective
methods - a problem with structuralism/introspectionism, the approach to studying the mind that
came before behaviorism (pg 8, textbook). While cognitivism acknowledges that introspection
cannot be the only source of information/evidence for studying the mind, the shift to cognitivism
was not sparked by the issues with structuralism. Option B is closer to the reason for the shift
because it mentions behaviorism’s focus on observable behavior dictating psychological
research, but cognitivism did not promote the exclusion of behavioral observation in
psychological study (pg. 10, textbook). This leads us to the correct answer, option C. Option C
is correct because cognitivism disputed the dominance of behaviorism (pg. 13, textbook). In
other words, cognitivism aimed to bring the study of the mind, peering into the “black box”,
back into psychological conversations, complementing behavioral observations (Slides 59, 78,
W1L1). Option D correctly identifies Tolman’s findings as important in the development of
cognitivism but misrepresents the findings of the experiment and confuses Tolman's work with
Skinner’s (pg. 11-12, textbook). Skinner’s experiments on operant conditioning were important
in the study of behaviorism and a cognitivist would argue that operant conditioning does not
fully substantiate the rats’ behavior, but a cognitivist would also not discount the same
behavioral observations Skinner made as useful information in the study of the rats’ minds.

Question 2 (Weeks 4-6)
In our textbook’s chapter on attention we discussed inattentional deafness and the cocktail party
effect. What is the main distinction between the cocktail party effect and inattentional deafness?
e.

The cocktail party effect demonstrates our ability to attend to one stimulus while
ignoring others - selective attention-, but inattentional deafness refers to how we can
choose to drown out auditory stimuli when specifically focused on visual tasks.
f. The cocktail party effect deals with the unconscious processing of relevant auditory
information whereas inattentional deafness refers to our ability to tune out irrelevant
auditory stimuli.
g. The cocktail party effect explains our ability to shift our attention between multiple
auditory stimuli, but inattentional deafness highlights how we fail to recognize auditory
stimuli while focused on a visual task.
h. The cocktail party effect refers to our ability to process auditory stimuli amidst
other competing auditory stimuli whereas inattentional deafness involves a failure to
notice auditory stimuli due to high load visual tasks.

Explanation:
This question aims to have the student recognize the difference between inattention and
selective attention. Also, Lavie’s load theory of attention is implicated in both of these
phenomena: for the cocktail party, a person’s ability to selectively attend will ultimately be
affected by the person’s cognitive load, and, for inattentional deafness, load theory helps explain
how we can be actively attending and unintentionally lack the perception of auditory stimuli
because our attentional load is already greatly occupied (pg. 99-101, textbook; Slide 3, 26
W5L2; Slide 49,50, W5L1).
Option E correctly identifies selective attention as the driver behind the cocktail party
effect but incorrectly defines inattentional deafness as something we can choose to do (pg. 95,
textbook). We do not choose to miss or drown out auditory stimuli while focusing on a visual
task - we miss perceiving auditory stimuli because of our filled attentional load (Slide 26,
W5L2). Option F touches on a caveat of the cocktail party effect instead of focusing on its actual
definition: unconscious processing of relevant information could be hearing your name at a party
while engrossed in a conversation you are having with your friend (Slide 9, W6L1). Once again,
this option mischaracterizes inattentional deafness as something we can choose to do. Option G
talks about shifting attention rather than selectively attending with regard to the cocktail party
effect but correctly characterizes inattentional deafness. Option H is the correct answer because it
provides correct definitions of both the cocktail party effect and inattentional deafness and
correctly identifies that they both tell us something about attention’s role in processing auditory
stimuli (pg. 96, 116-117, textbook).

Question 3 (Weeks 7-9)
Which of these neurological observations are associated with insight problem solving as opposed
to analytical problem solving?
i. Left hemispheric activation both temporally and spatially shown in EEG and fMRI scans
during tasks that require semantic integration.
j. Occipital alpha band EEG activity spikes right before insights make their way into
consciousness
k. A sudden and heightened activation of the anterior cingulate before arriving at a solution
born out of insight
l. Enhanced right hemispheric and diffuse visual cortex activity during resting state
Explanation:
This question revolves around week 9’s core reading, “The Cognitive Neuroscience of
Insight”, by Kounios and Beeman. To answer this question, students must think about the
different neurological events that occur before and during insight problem solving. Many of the

answers are misleading because they confuse either spatial or temporal information from
Kounios and Beeman’s findings.
Option I is a trap because it points out the hemispheric asymmetry in activation in insight
problem solving and also recognizes the dual EEG and fMRI approach described in the paper
(pg. 79, Kounios & Beeman). The primary flag in this option to signal that it is incorrect should
be “left”. It is right, not left, hemispheric activation that is shown during semantic integration and
processing, a processing and manipulation vital to insight (pg. 82, Kounios & Beeman). Option J
is correct and touches on the sudden appearance of insight into consciousness by using the word
“spike” rather than something like “gradually rise”. This finding is referenced by the “Attending
In, Out, and Around” section of Kounios and Beeman’s paper (p. 83). While the occipital alpha
activation is sudden, the anterior cingulate’s activation is not fully tied to a solution to a problem
arriving into consciousness. Option K is therefore incorrect because the greater activation of the
anterior cingulate is more involved with exploring nondominant solution possibilities (pg. 86,
Kounios & Beeman). Option L is incorrect because enhanced right hemispheric and diffuse
activity during resting state in the visual cortex is associated more with a person's susceptibility
for creative and insightful problem solving than it is actually having an insight. These findings
point towards insightful thinkers having a hemispheric bias but that isn’t to say that a person with
this enhanced resting state activation may not also solve a problem analytically (81-82, Kounios
& Beeman).

Part II
“Funes the Memorious”, a fictitious short story by Jorge Luis Borges, tells the story of a
man unable to categorize things in the world around him: if light shines on a blade of grass
causing it to shine a new color, Funes is unable to recognize and categorize that newly colored
blade of grass as the same blade he just laid eyes on moments earlier. In lecture, we discussed
how, in this story, Funes was hypothetically experiencing the world directly, rather than through
a mental representation or a world informed by concepts. Because of this, Funes is unable to
make inferences about the world around him - his ability to retrieve old memories, form
hypotheses, generalize or summarize events or situations, or even recognize people in a new pair
of glasses is impaired. In remembering absolutely everything as a new concept or object, Funes’
life arguably becomes much more difficult and complex to navigate (Slide 56, W8L1). Our
everyday lives are shaped by our ability to call on prior knowledge, and create new conceptual
knowledge in ways that are manageable for our minds - rationing cognitive resources, being
wary of cognitive load. Our expectations, derived from prior knowledge and experience, coupled
with our ability to categorize information and direct attention, serve as pivotal mechanisms that
inform our behavior and streamline our navigation of the world. These cognitive processes
intricately shape how we interpret, respond to, and interact with our environment, allowing a
more fulfilling engagement with the ever changing and complex world around us.
We cannot possibly interact with every stimuli presented in our environment. Helping us
avoid cognitive and sensory overload when interacting with others and our environment, our
ability to attend selectively to pertinent information (deemed pertinent by expectations and prior

knowledge), allows us to manage our cognitive load and navigate our environments effectively.
Selective attention refers to the process in which we focus on specific features in our
environment while filtering out distractions or irrelevant information. What we deem as
irrelevant or relevant is influenced by our expectations and knowledge: a study was conducted to
see if radiologists would notice that photos of gorillas were superimposed onto brain scans (Slide
27, W5L1). The radiologists were not expecting to find tumors in the region that the gorilla was
placed and therefore selectively attended to areas of the scan that they expected a tumor to be
found in. The results of this experiment found that many radiologists did not notice the gorillas
on the 3D lung scans - because they were selectively attending to another part of the scan, they
succumbed to inattentional blindness (Slide 21, W5L1). In lecture we discussed that this did not
make the radiologists bad doctors. If anything, they were so attentive to their job, finding tumors,
that any irrelevant information did not distract them. This ability to filter out task-irrelevant
stimuli is seen to be a product of the load theory of attention proposed by Lavie (pg. 99,
textbook). The radiologists were performing an arguably high load task, searching for tumors in
a brain scan, and therefore there was little attentional capacity left to notice things like a gorilla
in a place that was already left out in the radiologists’ selective attendance.
Attention is also instrumental in order to “experience a coherent world”, as mentioned by
our textbook (pg. 119). Attention is an important factor in perception and can help us bind the
features of an object to create our perception of it. We can look at the effect this has on our
ability to recognize objects by discussing Treisman’s feature integration theory (FIT) and an
individual suffering from Balint’s syndrome (pg. 121 textbook). Briefly, FIT says that objects are
broken into parts and those parts are categorized into features of the original object; then, the
features are combined through focused attention and this informs our perception of an object.

Patient R.M. had parietal lobe damage that took away his ability to focus attention on individual
objects - Balint’s syndrome. As a result, patient R.M was unable to correctly combine and later
ascribe an object’s features to the object - a red T and a blue O became a red O or a blue T.
Treisman’s experiments on her own theory also showed that prior knowledge was important for
categorizing and binding features of an object during the focused attention stage: in an illusory
conjunction experiment, similar to the one performed on patient R.MTreisman told the
participants what they were being shown. If she said the word “carrot”, the participants would
perceive the upside down orange triangle to be a carrot. Fitting the shapes’ features to a familiar
category allowed them to improve on the task and correctly identify the objects when prompted
(pg. 121, textbook)
Revisiting Jorge Luis Borges’ character, Funes, it’s interesting to think about how his
inability to use prior knowledge or make assumptions about objects cause Funes to have a nearly
perfect memory, a byproduct of his condition that Professor Heald mentioned in class. Are
imperfect or incomplete memories a good thing? One way to think about this “incomplete”
memory is to think about how some actors learn their lines for a play. Personally, I have a friend
who learns the first letter of every word in their script. Once they’ve memorized that, they write
it down and begin to remember what each letter stood for in a sentence - similar to a very long
acronym. This demonstrates chunking - a way of increasing the amount of information we are
able to store in our limited capacity memory (pg. 140, textbook; Slide 13, W6L2). Once the long
acronym was memorized, the retrieval of this acronym then brought the memory into working
memory where my friend could manipulate the information stored in the acronym to represent
the words in the script he was trying to memorize. This memory was incomplete in the sense that
he didn’t memorize every word of the script, but it did allow him to remember his lines faster

and, keeping in mind that memory has a limited capacity, more efficiently. While this story was
anecdotal and doesn’t exactly demonstrate turning small bits of information into more
meaningful units, it showed that we don’t need a perfect memory to remember the things we
want to remember. It would simply take a lot of work and rehearsal to just memorize a bunch of
words or digits on a page, but we have processes by which this rehearsal and encoding can
become easier - prior knowledge and expertise make storing lots of information in memory a
little bit easier. Ericsson conducted a digit memory study in which a patient, SF, was asked to use
chunking to recode digits into chunks that would make it easier for him to store in his memory.
SF’s chunking was improved by using his expertise as a runner to reprogram arbitrary digits he
was asked to memorize into running times. He retrieved prior knowledge from his long term
memory to increase the capacity of his short term memory system to hold up to 79 digits at a
time which he could then retrieve and decode in working and short term memory (pg. 141,
textbook). We also see expertise awarding memory advantages to experts in their fields; chess
masters are significantly better at memorizing playable non random chess boards because they
are able to chunk based on prior knowledge of the game and configurations of pieces (Slide 17,
W6L2). Chess masters are therefore able to solve problems in a game much more quickly and
efficiently than novices are because of their ability to retrieve information from long term stores
and use that information to chunk information effectively into their short term memory (pg. 375,
textbook). The core reading, “The Mind and Brain of Short-Term Memory”, also touches on how
chunked information can be maintained in short term memory through the focus of attention by
retrieval from LTM or perceptual encoding (pg. 212). Attention and prior knowledge constantly
intertwine to direct our perception, memory and other cognitive processes.

Revisiting our discussion of chess masters, the chess masters performed just as poorly as
the novices when board configurations were randomized. They weren’t able to call on prior
knowledge anymore which made producing a solution to their task just as difficult to find as for
anyone else. When the correct knowledge cannot be applied, solutions become harder to find.
The idea that prior knowledge can help direct and guide one’s attention towards the right place,
like in the gorilla brain scan experiment, and that solutions are found more easily when the
correct knowledge is applied, helps us understand how insights in problem solving can be found
(Slide 11, W9L2). In lecture, insight was described as the process of activating the appropriate
knowledge for a certain problem. For example, in Weisberg and Alba’s nine dot experiment, it is
not until participants are told they can draw lines outside of the box that the task of connecting
the dots is no longer confined to the shape the dots made (snapping out of functional fixedness).
Only with the right knowledge were a substantial, though not majority, amount of people able to
solve the problem (Slide 20, W9L2). SImilarly, in the out of focus pictures task, the pictures look
just like noise if you are given no context. As soon as someone is told that there is an elephant
somewhere in the image, the viewer begins to reframe their thinking and allow their mind to
entertain new possibilities for the image. While some may look for a tusk, big ears, and four feet,
others may suddenly see the whole elephant, all at once, after altering their expectation of what
the image contains (Slide 18, W9L2). While expectation can be a good thing for problem
solving, some consequences of knowledge and expectation, mental set and functional fixedness,
can actually make problem solving harder (pg. 360, textbook). In these cases, attentional release
can spark creativity and divergent thinking. This can be done through a myriad of methods taking a walk, meditating, showering, sleeping, and mind wandering (pg. 386-389, textbook).
While mind wandering can seem counterproductive to problem solving there is a neurological

evidence that there is a connection between mind wandering and creativity facilitated by the
default mode network (pg. 384-385, textbook). It’s hard to forget what you know, because
sometimes it’s all you can hang on to, but that release of attentional control can let your mind
reframe its view and go where you didn’t know you needed it!
