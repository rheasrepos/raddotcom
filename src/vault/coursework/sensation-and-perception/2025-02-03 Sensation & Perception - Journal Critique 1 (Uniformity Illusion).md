Journal Critique #1: The Uniformity Illusion
The article by Otten et al. (2017) investigates a novel visual illusion, called the
uniformity illusion, where peripheral stimuli appear to take on the characteristics of
central stimuli during prolonged fixation on the central stimuli. The specific hypothesis
tested is that detailed peripheral visual experiences are reconstructed by the brain
based on central stimuli, leading to an illusory perception of uniformity between the
central and peripheral visual fields. To test this hypothesis, the authors conducted eight
experiments, where participants fixated on central stimuli while peripheral stimuli were
either dissimilar or identical to the central stimuli. Participants were required to indicate
when the entire image they were viewing appeared uniform, with various visual features
manipulated, including shape, orientation, motion, pattern, identity, and luminance.
Results showed that participants frequently reported the uniformity illusion, with an
average of 83% of trials indicating perceived uniformity, and the illusion developed over
time, typically requiring at least 2 seconds of fixation on the central image. This means
that they reported that their entire visual field was uniform when, really, the peripheral
stimuli differed from the central stimuli. The uniformity illusion was found to be
influenced by the degree of similarity between the peripheral and central image: the
larger the difference between the two visual fields, the less likely participants are to
have illusory vision. The authors conclude that the brain's reconstruction of peripheral
stimuli based on central information results in a rich but illusory visual experience that
makes our visual field continuous and detailed despite the shortcomings of peripheral
vision. This study's significance lies in its demonstration of how conscious visual
perception may not accurately reflect sensory input, shedding light on the intricacies of
our visual processing system and how there exists disconnects between our brains’
representations and recreations of external stimuli.
While the study’s results are compelling and supported by the authors’
interpretation of their results, one issue with the study is the potential role of expectation
and priming in the experimental design. The experimental design asks participants to
fixate on a central stimulus while the peripheral image fades in over time. In some
sense, this fading process inadvertently establishes a baseline where the "normal" state
of the display is defined by the central image. Because participants are explicitly
instructed to report when the display appears uniform, they may be biased toward
seeing uniformity, as they expect a shift towards uniformity (the appearance of the
central image) to occur. Even if not on every trial, participants expected that they would
be asked to report a change towards uniformity in the periphery. Given this concern, a
statistic I would be interested in finding in the data is: how many participants never
reported uniformity? Or, maybe a better question is, should the researchers have
included something like a “placebo” trial? While one of the researchers' final
experiments awarded us assurance that people were responding truthfully and not
randomly, this does not rule out the possibility that participants were responding based

on an implicit expectation that peripheral stimuli should eventually match the central
ones, rather than purely on perceptual experience.
Furthermore, the choice to fade in the peripheral images rather than fade them
out was a puzzling choice to me. While we do not know if fading the periphery out would
have yielded different results than fading it in, I do wonder if the choice to fade the
periphery in skewed participants towards seeing the central image overall. I see this as
an issue of framing the study around illusory peripheral vision rather than as a change
detection problem. The study asks participants to indicate when the entire display
appears uniform, which implicitly assumes that the relevant variable is peripheral stimuli
becoming similar to the center. But, a more direct question might be: Do participants
notice that the periphery is changing at all?
It is clear that peripheral stimuli are part of the visual field, but the crucial
question is whether participants are detecting changes in the periphery, rather than
merely experiencing uniformity. With the central image shown first, it seems this study
primes participants to recognize the central vision image. For example, in the famous
gestalt rabbit-duck illusion, often, if people are told that the illusion is a rabbit the first
time they see it, they may only see a rabbit unless told that seeing a duck is an option.
In a way, it feels like we are convincing the participants of this study that there is a
change to be seen before they can realize it themselves. While I am not necessarily
proposing a fix for this issue, as maybe it is an issue that cannot be fixed due to the fact
that giving participants instructions is a necessary part of this study, I do propose a
modification to this study to make the data collection feel more complete. If we reversed
the framing of the task—for instance, asking participants to press a button when they
notice a change, rather than when they perceive uniformity– we may obtain a fuller
picture of just how the uniformity illusion presents.
Overall, Otten et al. demonstrate the uniformity illusion, showing that peripheral
stimuli can appear to take on the properties of central stimuli over time. While their
findings suggest that the brain reconstructs peripheral details based on the central
visual field, the study does not fully address the potential impact of expectation effects,
or cognitive priming which could contribute to illusory visions. Additionally, the way the
illusion was framed—as a shift toward uniformity rather than a change detection
task—may have influenced participants' responses. To strengthen the study’s claims,
future research should explore alternative experimental designs, such as reversing the
stimulus fading order or testing different priming conditions. Despite these limitations,
the study highlights the complexity of visual perception and begs for further investigation
into the mechanisms which power our conscious visual experience.
