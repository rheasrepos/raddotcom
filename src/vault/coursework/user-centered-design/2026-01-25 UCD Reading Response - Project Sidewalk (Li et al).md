Rhea Madhogarhia
“I never realized sidewalks were a big deal”: A Case Study of a
Community-Driven Sidewalk Audit Using Project Sidewalk - Li et. al.
Takeaways: The authors found that community-driven digital civics can successfully support
accessibility advocacy and education while driving pro-social behavioral change! A key finding was the
shift from addressing infrastructure issues remedially (fixing individual problems) to approaching them
systemically through community efforts and existing community organizations (Girl Scouts). By
distributing work among community members, the study showed that digital tools can facilitate
engagement across all age groups and lead to significant knowledge gains and care regarding urban
accessibility. Strengths: A major strength of the project is the tool design itself, which features an
easy-to-use, interactive, color-coded map that serves as both a crowdsourced data collector and a resource
for information seekers. I thought the use of “mapathons” was a smart and fun design choice that gathered
large amounts of data quickly, while re-auditing processes helped ensure data quality, something that
tends to be hard with subjective coding of data. Weaknesses: Because the tool is a visual search and
labeling tool, it can be inaccessible to people who are blind, have low vision, or have upper-body motor
disabilities (think even carpal tunnel). Limitations and Questions: Findings may not be representative of
other communities that don’t have the same resources or sense of community (like having a Girl Scouts
troop). Participation in using the tools also required a laptop, mouse, and internet connection, which could
be a barrier in low-income communities. One overall question I have is, is advocacy legitimized by a
digital tool? Does the use of technology make the advocacy seem more “professional” or valid to city
councils compared to traditional manual audits? Unfortunately, I think it’s easier to pitch change with data
and a product to back it up than to state why an issue matters to you specifically.
Interesting Parts: The part of the paper I found most interesting was the involvement of the Girl Scouts
and the use of gamification to keep volunteers engaged. It was surprising to see how relevant the work
became for participants: mapathons were fun enough that people continued contributing even after the
events ended! I also thought the study did a good job of including the target population in the design
process by having able-bodied scouts interact with community members with disabilities. Furthermore,
the lack of consensus on what the most important accessibility issue is led data insights and visualizations
to be crucial in deciding next steps to bring to the City Council. I also think that data exploration for the
Girl Scouts really sparked their interest and determination to make a change. Applications: This study
reminds us that when designing inclusive technology, prioritizing user agency can be rewarding. I think
recruiting naturally interested parties results in having invested volunteers contributing higher quality data
(even if reaudits are performed). Furthermore, this was a good example of how to turn user-centered
design projects into actionable advocacy planning (city council presentation).
