Rhea Madhogarhia
Reading Response #2: HelpCall: Designing Informal Technology Assistance for Older
Adults via Videoconferencing - Tanprasert et. al
Researchers from the University of British Columbia introduce "HelpCall," a video-conferencing (VMC)
augmentation designed to assist older adults (OAs) with computer tasks by creating step-by-step visual
guides during synchronous support sessions. Through a qualitative study, the researchers compared
TOOLTIP and LIST against standard Zoom. They found that while both designs improved user
confidence and independence compared to the baseline, the TOOLTIP design was generally preferred;
one of TOOLTIP’s strengths was its ability to scaffold and allow the OA to learn the task on their own
(think of “teaching a man to fish” rather than buying him one). A strength of the study design was the
participant screening that made sure seniors had a baseline proficiency in computer usage to measure the
interface's impact accurately. But, the authors admit this is also a limitation, since the participants were a
"socioeconomically advantaged group", potentially skewing the results away from OAs with lower digital
literacy. One main question I have concerns the initiation of help: the authors note that existing
professional services like MicroMentor require detailed digital requests, so how do we resolve the
dilemma where the method of requesting help is just as inaccessible as the task the user needs help with?
I’m specifically reminded of the example we discussed in class, where the target audience to sell World
Cup tickets to was also an audience that wasn’t used to buying tickets online.
I thought it was exciting that the researchers explicitly discussed the emotional burden and feelings of
OAs, specifically noting how reluctance to be a "burden" on family members often stops them from
seeking help. I think this shows the value of HCI’s combination of qualitative and quantitative data. It
highlights that having agency and support over the use of the technology itself is impactful, rather than
just getting the task done. I think this informs inclusive design by exposing the "barriers to entry" in
current VMC tools; if requesting help requires navigating digital forms foreign to users, we are probably
failing the users who need it the most. Overall, I think inclusive design sometimes needs to "go analog" or
simplify the initial request phase to actually be accessible to this demographic. I wonder what the analog
of offering a service like this would look like: flyers? Newspaper listings? Phonebooks?
