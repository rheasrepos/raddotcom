Rhea Madhogarhia
Reading Response #4
Reading Response: Exploring Sound Awareness in the Home for People who are Deaf or Hard of
Hearing – Jain et al.
Summary: This study investigates how Deaf and hard-of-hearing (DHH) individuals perceive and
manage sounds at home, using this insight to design and test three sound awareness prototypes (a list, a
floor plan, and a waveform). The researchers identified that participants had a strong preference for the
floorplan design because of its intuitive spatial visualization and room specificity. Still, the list format was
also valued for providing a historical record of home noises, which could potentially help participants put
sounds in context. A strength of the paper and a section of the paper I found particularly
unique/interesting was the establishment of a “design space” early in the process (made me think of set
theory, linear algebra, domains, etc), yet the study was limited by a small sample size where most
participants lived alone, a limitation because this could potentially fail to capture the dynamics of having
roommates. My main questions revolve around the methodology and participant motivations. I am unsure
what the specific criteria for "thematic saturation" are, and I didn’t find that term to be well-defined. I also
wonder what the design process was for selecting the specific thematic scenarios used in the
Wizard-of-Oz part of the study. Finally, the paper mentions a preference for form factors that are not
easily recognized as assistive technology… I wonder why invisibility is prioritized for participants; is this
an aesthetic choice, or is it an effort to avoid social stigma or burdening others around you?
Reaction: One thing I found compelling about this study was how ease of use seemed to dictate the entire
study; the researchers prioritized glanceability and intuitive visualizations over complex data (supported
by the lack of support for the waveform prototype), which resonated with participants' desire for
immediate, actionable information. The varying preferences for notifications—specifically the distinction
between "need to know" actionable sounds versus background noise—were also thought-provoking. I
found this interesting because in our Group Project, I am wondering how to choose notification frequency
and style. To apply these findings to inclusive technology design, I would focus on the loudness and
cost-effectiveness aspects mentioned by participants. For example, since loudness perception can be
subjective or hard to visualize, I would design a sort of calibration step where a trusted hearing person
helps the DHH user define what constitutes "loud" versus "soft" in their specific environment.
