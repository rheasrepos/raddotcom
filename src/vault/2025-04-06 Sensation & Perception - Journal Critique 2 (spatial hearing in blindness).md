Journal Article Critique #2
In a study titled “General Enhancement of Spatial Hearing in Congenitally Blind
People”, Battal et al. attempt to address how congenital blindness affects the
development of spatial hearing abilities compared to sighted individuals. Interested in
the contrast between previous research that showed that “visually deprived animals”
show less precise sound localization skills and, conversely, that congenitally blind
people have improved localization acuity, these researchers aimed to settle these
conflicting findings. To make this study as robust as possible and make a more
conclusive finding than previous studies, Battal et al. designed a multidirectional
psychophysical minimum audible angle task with 17 congenitally blind and 17 sighted
participants to create a fine-grained psychophysical mapping of the global surrounding
space for all their participants. They found individual participants' threshold values
(degrees of angle) in the horizontal, vertical, frontal, rear, central, and peripheral spaces
from which sounds could originate. To do this, researchers instructed blindfolded
participants to compare positions of two sounds from an array of 61 speakers using a
“forced-choice” response ("left/right" or "up/down"). Then, using linear mixed effects
models (LMMs), researchers assessed group differences. Across participants, distance
from the speaker and head position were controlled. Analysis of the collected data and
the results presented in Figure 2 demonstrated that blind participants outperformed
sighted participants in all spatial conditions, especially in peripheral and vertical spaces.
Furthermore, both groups seemed to struggle more with rear space localization, but
blind individuals showed less impairment than sighted individuals. As the title of the
study suggests, the authors concluded that congenital blindness leads to generalized
enhancement of spatial hearing, suggesting vision is not essential for calibrating
auditory spatial skills, unlike some previous research may suggest.
While these findings are the product of robust and careful experimental design
that supports the hypothesis that congenitally blind individuals have enhanced spatial
hearing abilities, I am unsure of whether or not this is the only interpretation of the
presented results. It is still unclear, after reading this study, whether or not congenitally
blind individuals have enhanced spatial hearing because they lack a visual stimulus or
because they have deliberately practiced their auditory spatial skills. While the
discussion section of this paper touches on this (“perceptual training effects”), it does so
by saying further research on the neuroplasticity of congenitally blind individuals must
be done instead of discussing the implications the validity of this claim may have on this
paper’s findings. It’s commonly known that some blind people learn to echolocate (some

do this with clicks, hums, or by tapping canes), and it’s not far-fetched at all to think that
blind individuals may go through auditory training. If the researchers had surveyed
participants on whether or not they had this kind of trained expertise, the results of this
study could have more clearly told us whether or not enhanced spatial hearing is due to
a neuroplasticity response from the brain from the lack of a visual stimulus or a trained
response. If most of the blind participants had gone through formal training or practice
for auditory localization, then the sighted individuals in this study would have been at an
experiential disadvantage for this experiment.
Furthermore, it seems reasonable to assume that sighted individuals are more
unfamiliar with auditory-only tasks. Thus, being blindfolded and being asked to respond
to only an auditory stimulus seems as though it could be a more disorienting and
confusing task for a sighted individual than a congenitally blind individual; thus, I would
be interested in learning about how difficult the task felt for each group of participants.
While it makes intuitive sense that taking a way a sensory input from sighted individuals
would make this task harder for them than for individuals that have lived without that
sensory input since birth, this task may also be harder for sighted individuals if there is
an emotional response to a lack of vision, or if part of their “cognitive load” was being
occupied by “thinking harder” or concentrating/attending more due to a lack of visual
stimulus. While this is a speculative point to make, if the researchers were to take these
issues into account, there may have been differences in experimental design or data
collection that may have affected their results and discussion.
Finally, I would be interested in whether or not this study could be replicated with
more naturalistic sounds, like speech, nature noises, or just general environmental
noise (e.g., traffic). Being asked to respond to pink noise, which was used in the study,
is not representative of real-world hearing. And, if participants had gone through
perceptual training, then the lack of diversity or ecological validity of the stimuli would be
familiar to them, possibly contributing to higher performance on the task. One possible
experiment to confirm Batal et al. ’s findings using real-world stimuli would be to test the
cocktail party effect on both congenitally blind and sighted participants. Following the
findings of this study, I predict that congenitally blind participants would be able to locate
where their name was being called from more accurately than sighted participants.
Overall, Battal et al.'s study persuasively presents a methodological approach
and findings that suggest that congenital blindness enhances auditory spatial
localization, challenging the notion that vision is necessary for calibrating spatial
hearing. However, the findings leave open whether this enhancement stems from
neuroplasticity or deliberate auditory training since the study didn't account for
participants' prior experiences with solely auditory stimuli. Future research should
incorporate naturalistic sounds and survey participants' auditory training history to better
understand how spatial hearing can be enhanced.
