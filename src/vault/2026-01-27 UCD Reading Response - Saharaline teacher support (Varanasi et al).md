Rhea Madhogarhia

Reading Response: “Saharaline: A Collective Social Support Intervention for Teachers in
Low-Income Indian Schools” - Varanasi et al.
Summary: Main Takeaways: The paper presents Saharaline, a hybrid WhatsApp-based intervention that
successfully engaged teachers in low-income Indian schools by connecting them with a decentralized
collective of local caseworkers and remote experts for social support. They demonstrated that a collective
approach effectively surfaced and addressed a wide range of stressors for educators. Strengths: I think a
strength of this project is the use of WhatsApp, an existing technology which is hugely popular in India
(speaking from personal and family experience). These researchers leveraged existing, already
widespread technologies to expand a social support network beyond teachers' immediate communities something important considering they were helping low-resource schools. The matching service design
(teachers, to caseworkers, to experts), which drew workers from different organizations with a wide
breadth of expertise, was also a strength because it was able to address a wide array of teacher issues.
Limitations/Weaknesses: The intervention placed a heavier load on the “backend systems” (caseworkers
and experts), and power differentials still existed, even if there was a concerted effort to mitigate that; I
was particularly surprised that the experts wanted distance from teachers. Furthermore, the translation of
data from local languages may have introduced some loss of nuance, and given that this research was
being conducted in India, I don’t necessarily see the need to translate before analysis. Also, the sample
size was relatively small, and the researchers call for longer and more widespread studies in their
conclusion. Main Questions: Firstly, if smartphones already bring work outside of work hours for these
teachers, does this intervention worsen that problem? Also, it's confusing that the support is mostly
emotional help—is this meant to substitute for therapy? I wonder if these supporters are trained on when
to report issues to a crisis line, especially given cultural attitudes around mental health (although my view
on this may be biased towards my extended family and experiences in India). Additionally, I wonder what
the training for supporters entails—for people who are implicitly aware of their problems, I bet a
constantly affirming and positive supporter is necessary to get them to share more and continue building
trust.
Reaction: What I Found Interesting: I thought that the use of WhatsApp was a really smart and
intuitive choice for this design. Furthrmoe I found the collective design process with NGOs an impactful
design choice, and the differentiation between implicit and explicit issues that teachers brought to the
helpline particularly insightful to think about for a textline type tool. Applicability to Inclusive Tech:
From this paper, I think we learn about how to build on existing, scalable platforms that people already
use to lower barriers to adoption. Second, the paper provides insights into how to design for low-resource
settings where infrastructure and personnel may be limited. Lastly, thinking about how to reduce the
burden of being a supporter while increasing the depth and breadth of knowledge that comes from
supporters can help reduce burdens on individuals within support networks. More Reaction: Overall, I
thought this paper had an ambitious project and achieved its goals with careful consideration and design
for the communities they were building for!
