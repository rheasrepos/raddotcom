Rhea Madhogarhia

Shani’s quote about sensory systems’ ability to help organisms cope and adapt with
changing conditions in their environments is directly supported by Oliva and Torralba’s work on
the role of context in object recognition. One way to rephrase Shani’s view is that our sensory
systems do not necessarily help us perceive an objective reality, but instead help categorize and
process the world in a way that makes sense or is consistent with the experience we’ve had in
our lives thus far. Saffran’s speech processing experiment, found in the textbook, which involved
recording how attentive infants were to certain sounds and demonstrating statistical learning
and transitional probabilities in language learning supports Shani’s quote, which overall
describes a top down processing approach; the infants paid more attention to words they didn’t
already know or were less familiar with, showing that their processing systems were helping
them adapt more to learning the novel sounds , “changing conditions” to Shani, rather than the
ones they were most familiar with.
Our processing systems’ ability to deal and make sense of novel information almost
instantaneously is also demonstrated by our ability to use contextual information to correctly or
conveniently identify objects. In the paper, Oliva and Torralba state: “Even if objects can be
identified by intrinsic infor- mation, context can simplify the object discrimination by decreasing
the number of object categories, scales and positions that must be considered” (525). They then
go on to say that a statistical summary of the elements in a scene further help us to
contextualize and identify an object. An example that clearly supports this is the blurred blob in
four different backgrounds: “the multiple personalities of a blob”. Although intrinsically, the
blurred image could potentially be identified as a shoe, or visually just a blob or pile of slop, if
shown without a scene, when placed on a street or a road, it assumes the form of a car in our
minds because of its context. It is more convenient for us to recognize the shoes as a car and a
person because it doesn’t make sense or really serve a human to believe or perceive that a
giant shoe is being placed on a road or walking down the street. Using the scene to categorize
the blob in different scenarios serves us well because it helps us to maintain an internal
consensus of perception and allows us to keep making categories that help us process the
world. Being able to assign visual signals we receive to objects is definitely something we that
we take for granted and the expectation and experiences we bring to our daily lives, an
important part of understanding top down processing, is important for our own mental
stability/reinforcement of learned mental categories/organization and ability to survive and
navigate the world.
